Company

Qunomedical is a Berlin based medical startup. Qunomedical is a digital health platform that connects patients with doctors and hospitals worldwide.

Problem

We lacked consistency and quality in our product. Wording, UI, colors, buttons, text styles, etc. were out of sync and based on a past designers and their preferences and goals. A design system built with react and based on a single Figma file would fix this. The goal: To make it easier for designers and developers to build products. If it fails at this, you need to change it.

Goals

  • To make it easier for designers and developers to build products.
  • Create a Design System that reflects Qunomedical branding, make it scalable in order to accommodate brand, product, component, and ensure all teams at Qunomedical can make effective usage of it. Help maintain consistency, be effective, scalable and improve collaboration.
  • Create documentation where everyone can find the official and foundational resources, principles, assets and other resources.
  • Overall, the design system will help Qunomedical establish a more efficient and effective design process, resulting in better user experiences and a stronger brand identity. Given that Qunomedical serves people across different countries and cultures, this is especially critical.

My Role

I strive to support the business, patients, and product team (PMs and Engineers). To do this I needed to build a reusable system of design patterns, components, and documentation guidelines.

Context

I've worked on and developed numerous design systems, but not from a company with some much legacy interfaces and experiences. I knew many of the best practices, but never built one that would be leveraged by multiple teams, developers or product managers. I leaned strongly into the Atomic Design Model and also best practices from companies like Shopify (Polaris Design System).

What did I inherit?

Where it started

With the rough strategy of, "most reused elements first" followed by "multi-page elements" following, I got started auditing

I screenshots of every page, modal, menu, and element in the web app and added the screenshots to Figma pages. I believe in working faster and not harder, so I didn't re-create anything until after we had a feel of the most inconsistent elements. Though it was buttons, inputs, and colors.

Audit

Once I believed I had cataloged every button, icon, and design pattern, I organized them into categories. A side-by-side examination revealed the pressing need for a design system. There were numerous button styles, inconsistent font weights, and scattered single-use icons.

Edit

This proved to be the most time-consuming part of the project. I had to make decisions regarding naming conventions, categorization, organization, and presentation. Much effort went into naming and re-naming my Sketch layers and organizing the symbols page. (Pro tip: Consider using Sketch plugins designed for these tasks.)

Continuous evolution

Design Systems, by their very nature, are dynamic living things. I established regular reminders to update, review, and share the style guide. This ongoing process ensures that the design system remains relevant and effective.

Results

As I mentioned in the introduction, my design process was slow, arduous, and unproductive. Working within an Agile team, I struggled to maintain agility. Initially, proposing the creation of a design system was met with skepticism and resistance. However, over time, we gained the necessary support.

Benefits:

The advantages of implementing a design system were significant:

  • Time-saving: We streamlined our workflow and reduced repetitive tasks.
  • UI consistency: Our design system helped us maintain a cohesive and trustworthy brand image.
  • Reducing complexity and ambiguity: It clarified design elements and improved user experience.
  • Collaboration and consensus: The design system fostered better teamwork and decision-making.
  • Foundation for improvement: It served as a solid basis for continuous enhancement.
  • In summary, we believed that a design system would make us more efficient and effective.
For a comprehensive list of benefits, you can refer to resources available on organizing design systems.
Results:

Creating an inventory of UI elements proved to be the most time-consuming phase, next to the editing process. Some of the challenges I encountered included:

Organizing elements effectively.

  • Establishing consistent naming conventions.
  • Capturing different states and interactions.
  • Monitoring progress and updates.
  • Creating dynamic, nested symbols for reusability.
  • Adhering to Atomic Design Principles.
  • Maintaining focus and motivation.

I also experienced a significant setback when my computer crashed, resulting in the loss of several hours of work. This incident prompted me to adopt a more proactive approach, committing to frequent backups and version control for our design files."

Die Anatomie - Preview

Qunomedical's design team was split between different departments and deliverables including interfaces, marketing landing pages, social media posts, promotional videos, patient documents, and much more. There was no single owner of the design system and we lacked a single source of truth.

My initiative at Qunomedical was to start developing a single design system, Die Anatomie. Die Anatomie, or Anatomie, ended up allowing all teams members to use to create consistent experiences for patients. Ranges of projects crossed all departments of Qunomedical. Die Anatomie was based on Brand Foster's Atomic Design principles and methodologies.

We like to know we are building useful cells (Atoms) and organs (Organisms) for non-design team members to use, each one able to control and slightly alter visual components in one way or another.
